I recently read a couple of articles on the topic of technical coaching and training (cf. links at the end). What struck me was that all of the methodologies that were introduced are very close to what I learned as an English teacher. The more I started to think about it, the more parallels I found. I thought I'd share them here, with the added bonus that I can come back to the list and remind me.

About ten years ago I received training for a CELTA, a Cambridge Certificate in English Language Teaching to Adults. This course is for people with little or no prior experience in teaching. It introduces a handful of basic concepts and recipes to plan student-centred lessons and deliver them in an engaging way. Certificate holders are thus enabled to teach ESL (English as a second language) at all levels. It does not cover things like curriculum planning and gives very little information on the theoretical background.

Some of the parallels are rather obvious: Language teachers “grade” their language - they adapt the vocabulary, speed, and sentence structure to match the level the students are at. This translates easily to grading exercises - use simpler Katas for beginners, and more complex ones for advanced groups. It can also mean planning a series of trainings in order of increasing difficulty. Pedro generously published the sequence that proved successful for him. In such a scenario of multiple trainings, it is very important to at least periodically repeat previous learnings. Ideally, one topic builds on the previous one, so repetitions are automatically built into the new topics.

One of the most important concepts was the ratio between STT and TTT - student talk time and teacher talk time. The scale should be very heavily on the students' side - the less a teacher talks, the better. It makes a lot of sense in the setting of language learning: trainees need to practice their skills, the more they talk, the better. But it holds true in other contexts as well. Listening is an entirely passive action. Actually doing something leads to better retention of information. As Emily Bache puts it: “You want to … get them to be active participants in their own learning”. She suggests giving participants code to review or letting them search for code snippets online.

The technique she describes is known as “guided discovery” in ESL (English as a Second Language) circles: The trainer prepares materials that help students discover the target grammar or vocabulary by themselves, asking questions more than telling them. The technical counterpart to this is preparing code samples that contain exactly the problem you wish to discuss. Pedro Santos stresses how important it is to taylor katas to the problem that is the focus of the session. It is seen as one of the harder and more time intensive ways of planning a lesson, but I have found it to often be the most rewarding ones, both for teachers/trainers as for participants.

A different formula for lesson planning is known as Test-Teach-Test: Students are asked to do an exercise that contains the target language - new grammar or vocabulary. In a next stage that new language is explained. Finally, the students do the same or a very similar exercise again, applying the new knowledge. “This technique works wonders”, writes Pedro. He uses the same Kata before and after theoretical imput. Comparing the first solution to the second is a perfect segue into contemplation of the learnings.
